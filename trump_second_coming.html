Trump Second Coming: Implications on Non-U.S. Dollar Stable Coins

21 August 2024 | Posts

Pearl Ramson

As President-elect Donald Trump prepares to return to the helm for the second time, analysts are examining the potential ramifications across various sectors of American life - from immigration to national security and the economy.

In this article, the mission is to explore how Trump’s policies and leadership might influence the burgeoning market of non-USD stable coins on his second tenure in office.

The Rise of New Asset-Backed Stable Coins

There is noticeable shift in global financial priorities as non-USD stable coins gain momentum. More than 15 non-USD stable coins have emerged within the past five years alone. Either they are tied to alternative currencies or assets, these digital assets offer traders a reliable anchor amidst crypto market turbulence.

There may be several factors driving the shift towards non-USD stable coins, but one prominent factor has always been “de-dollarization” trend globally.

For whatever reason, many countries have made moves to boycott the U.S. dollar in international payment settlement. And it seems the boycott is now extending beyond fiat U.S. dollar to digital U.S. dollar too.

Many nations, especially within the BRICS bloc (Brazil, Russia, India, China, South Africa), are developing their own payment systems and currencies, in what signals a push away from reliance on the dollar.

One prominent factor for de-dollarization is the risk of sanctions. The use of the US dollar in global trade has often exposed countries to the risk of US sanctions.

For instance, after the US confiscated Russia's foreign reserves, many countries who fear similar repercussions have been reconsidering holding their assets in dollars. Countries like China and Russia have increasingly traded in their national currencies to avoid these risks since then.

The future of the U.S. dollar at the moment equally looks uncertain. From many indications, many countries’ USD reserves have fallen greatly to an unprecedented height. Even though USD still remains the greenback in international settlement, there is a fear it may collapse soon due to its instability and uncertain future.

De-dollarization that is greatly impacting fiat-USD is now extending to its digital counterpart. Asides the “bi-polar world order” echoed by Russian president, Vladimir Putin, and the weaponization of USD, overreaching U.S. regulations, too, has open pathway for alternatives less influenced by U.S. policy decisions.

Financial autonomy and stability is becoming paramount in this era and non-USD digital assets offer just the solution.

Non-Dollar Stable Coins in a Glimpse

In the past five years, several non-USD stable coins, tied to either a fiat currency or physical assets, have emerged in response to the growing demand for alternatives to USD-backed cryptocurrencies.

Among them are:

1. Stasis Euro (EURS)

  • Launch Year: 2018
  • Peg: Euro (€)
  • Details: Developed by STASIS, EURS is backed by actual euro reserves. It is popular in European markets and audited regularly to ensure transparency. EURS facilitates payments, remittances, and transactions within the euro ecosystem.

2. Euro Coin (EUROC)

  • Launch Year: 2022
  • Peg: Euro (€)
  • Details: Issued by Circle, the creators of USDC, EUROC follows a full-reserve model, ensuring stability via euro-denominated bank accounts.

3. Tether Euro (EURT)

  • Launch Year: 2020
  • Peg: Euro (€)
  • Details: A euro-backed stable coin from Tether, leveraging Tether’s infrastructure to provide liquidity and accessibility similar to USDT.

4. XSGD (StraitsX Singapore Dollar)

  • Launch Year: 2020
  • Peg: Singapore Dollar (SGD)
  • Details: Issued by StraitsX, XSGD supports stable digital transactions and is widely used in Singapore and Southeast Asia for blockchain-based payments.

5. TRYB (BiLira)

  • Launch Year: 2020
  • Peg: Turkish Lira (TRY)
  • Details: A fiat-backed stable coin popular in Turkey, TRYB enables access to DeFi platforms and local financial ecosystems.

6. TrueGBP (TGBP)

  • Launch Year: 2019
  • Peg: British Pound Sterling (£)
  • Details: Created by TrustToken, TGBP is backed by GBP held in escrow, ensuring transparency and regulatory compliance.

7. Digital Yuan (e-CNY)

  • Launch Year: 2020 (Pilot stage)
  • Peg: Chinese Yuan (CNY)
  • Details: Issued by the People’s Bank of China, e-CNY is a state-backed digital currency. While not a typical stable coin, it provides a stable digital alternative for domestic and international transactions.

8. Celo Euro (cEUR)

  • Launch Year: 2021
  • Peg: Euro (€)
  • Details: Built for the Celo blockchain, cEUR facilitates global mobile payments, enabling efficient and low-cost transactions.

9. BRZ (Brazilian Real Token)

  • Launch Year: 2019
  • Peg: Brazilian Real (BRL)
  • Details: BRZ promotes cryptocurrency adoption in Brazil and is widely used for trading on local and global exchanges.

10. JPY Stable coin (JasmyCoin)

  • Launch Year: 2021
  • Peg: Japanese Yen (JPY)
  • Details: Focused on blending stable value with advanced technology, JasmyCoin supports IoT data privacy and management.

11. Gold-Backed Stable coins

  • Details: Examples like Paxos Gold (PAXG) tie their value directly to physical gold reserves, offering an alternative to fiat-backed stable coins and addressing inflation concerns.

Trump’s Stance on Cryptocurrencies

During Trump’s first administration (2017 - 2021), the crypto sector faced significant scrutiny. Trump himself publicly criticized cryptocurrencies, including Bitcoin, in 2019, calling them volatile and tools for illegal activity. This stance shaped global discourse on digital assets and led to stricter regulatory approaches.

Key events during Trump’s term include the SEC lawsuit against Ripple Labs (XRP) in 2020.

In December 2020, the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) filed a lawsuit against Ripple Labs to accuse it of conducting an unregistered securities offering worth $1.3 billion via its XRP token.

This case shook the crypto industry and spotlighted regulatory uncertainty. The SEC's stance was seen as part of a broader crackdown on cryptocurrencies during Trump administration.

Ripple argued that XRP should not be classified as a security, and this opened the door for a debate that continued into subsequent administrations and impacted the token's market value and adoption.

Many observers have viewed Trump appointment of crypto-skeptic officials as a move that stifles adoption and innovation.

Key figures during Trump’s administration like Treasury Secretary, Steven Mnuchin, also expressed similar skepticism. And, just like Trump, he equated them to tools for illicit activity.

Mnuchin, on his own part, pushed for tighter controls of the crypto sector through regulation. Regulation during the time was largely fragmented and ambiguous due to lack of robust framework to regulate the industry.

Part of the regulations the administration pushed for was the stringent Anti-Money Laundering laws targeting cryptocurrencies. This included the Financial Crimes Enforcement Network (FinCEN)'s proposal requiring cryptocurrency exchanges to report large transactions and identify personal wallet owners.

Critics argued that Trump’s fragmented and aggressive regulatory approach stifled innovation and hindered privacy in the crypto space.

Following this was the prioritized enforcement of crypto tax compliance, requiring taxpayers to disclose crypto holdings and transactions, while the Internal Revenue Service (IRS) was to step in to penalize non-compliance.

The Fate of Non-USD Stable Coins in Trump’s Second Term

If Trump returns to office, his administration’s stance on cryptocurrencies and non-USD stable coins is likely to remain stringent, driven by three key factors:

1. Crypto Skepticism: Trump’s previous public skepticism toward cryptocurrencies suggests he may maintain a hostile regulatory posture.

2. Historical Precedents: Trump’s previous measures contributed to the demise of Libra (now Diem), Meta’s ambitious stable coin project.

3. Preserving USD Hegemony: Trump’s push to safeguard the dominance of the U.S. dollar will likely result in stricter policies against international settlements involving non-USD currencies or stable coins. Reports have even suggested Trump may impose a 100% tariff on countries bypassing the USD for trade settlements.

If these predictions hold, Trump’s second term could introduce renewed regulatory crackdowns on stable coins and cryptocurrency markets. Non-USD stable coins, despite their rising importance in a multipolar economic order, may face significant hurdles in an environment that prioritizes USD dominance.
