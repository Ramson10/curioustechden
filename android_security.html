Android Security: Spotting and Avoiding Fake Apps That Hijack Your Phone and Steal Data

03 October 2024 |

Pearl Ramson

Meet my gaming buddy, Prosper. He is a game lover whose mobile phone nearly crashed after installing a suspicious game. An adventurous app enticed him so much that he decided to give it a try.

For him, the game was a bang – and indeed, it was an unforgettable one. From now on, no one needs to advise him why he needs to verify every apps before installing them, even if they come from trusted platforms.

It so happened that the so-called adventurous game mimicked one of his favorite titles. Unfortunately, Prosper granted the app all requested permissions without second thought.

From that moment, he lost control of his phone. Ads began to pop up incessantly. His phone screen remained lit indefinitely. Phone temperature surged alarmingly. He couldn’t enjoy his new game – or his phone.

He literally conceded the ownership of his phone to a faceless, unknown perpetrator at another end. And he owes all the blame.

He felt hopeless, ready to discard his phone in frustration. Poor him.

But what landed him in this mess?

The answer: he granted all requested access to a malicious, cloned app.

What You Should Know About Access and Permission on Android devices

Android devices rely on a permission-based system to regulate what apps can do on your phone. Permissions are requests made by apps to access specific features or data, such as the camera, microphone, contacts, or location.

For example, a camera app needs permission to use your camera, or a navigation app requires access to your location.

However, malicious apps often abuse this system by requesting unnecessary permissions. A fake calculator app, for instance, may ask for camera or microphone access—permissions irrelevant to its functionality.

Fake App: Social Engineering Tactics You Should Know

In cybersecurity, social engineering refers to the manipulative tactics attackers use to trick people into actions that benefit the attacker. It may come in many forms but the goal is to coerce, deceive or persuade people to do what the attacker wants.

From their trick is to exploit human emotions such as greed, sympathy, curiosity, etc. to influence their targets.

For example, Prosper was lured into installing a malicious app that mimicked his favorite adventurous game. Excited, he granted all requested permissions without question—a classic case of social engineering.

One of its recent trend is attacker exploiting the popularity of trending mobile apps by creating fraudulent versions. They build these apps to mimic legitimate apps with similar names, logos, and sometimes user interfaces.

This is comparable to a phishing attack where an attacker clones legitimate site for malicious purposes.

In 2023, Minecraft was maliciously cloned on Google Play Store. More than 35 million download had gone before it was discovered and taken down.

Attackers often use fake apps as a social engineering tactics to lure users into downloading them with promises of extra features or discounts. Depending on their intent, they may use these apps to install spyware on their targets’ device, redirect users to phishing sites, or lock the device with ransomware.

Sometimes, the intent behind these fake apps is to direct traffic through targets’ device to conceal attackers’ identity. Some VPN apps are notable for this kind of trick.

How to Spot Fake Apps

Research the developer:

Always research the developers behind the app. Legitimate developers often have verified accounts and multiple apps on app stores. Also ensure your research goes beyond the record of developers on app store. See it they have legit website or other contact details.

Read reviews:

Always read reviews about the app. Reviews are not time-waster but reported experience of other users about the app. Check to see complaints about bugs, crashes, or suspicious behavior.

Check download counts:

SCheck if the app has many downloads, especially if the app is identical with another one. Popular apps typically have millions of downloads. Be cautious of apps with low download counts.

Inspect permissions:

As a rule of thumb, always inspect permissions that app request for. And always avoid apps requesting permissions unrelated to their functionality. For instance, a calculator app asking for camera access.

Look for typos:

Check for typos if there is another identical app. Fake apps often have slight misspellings in their name or description.

Protective Measures

1. Stick to Google Play Store, but remain vigilant even there.

2. Enable Google Play Protect, for it scans apps for malware and alerts users of potential risks.

3. Install a Mobile Security Tools that can monitor, detect and block malicious apps, and monitor your mobile traffic in real-time.

4. Avoid downloading APKs from unverified websites.

5. Keep your Android OS and apps updated to patch vulnerabilities.

Be Vigilant

Even with all these measures in place, you still need to be vigilant. Never lower your guard. Once the promises of an app seem too good to be true then it is not – most especially free apps with premium competitors. If they are not selling something, chances are you are the product.

Once again, be vigilant.
